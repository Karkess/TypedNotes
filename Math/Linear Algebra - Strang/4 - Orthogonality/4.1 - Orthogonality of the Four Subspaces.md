# Chapter 4 - Orthogonality 
## 4.1 - Orthogonality of the Four Subspaces.

1. Orthogonal Vectors have $\vec{v}\,^\text{T}\,\vec{w}=0$. Then $\|\vec{V}\|^2+\|\vec{w}\|^2=\|\vec{v}+\vec{w}\|^2=\|\vec{v}=\vec{w}\|^2$.
2. Subspaces $\mathbb{V}$ and $\mathbb{W}$ are orthogonal when $\vec{v}\,^\text{T}\,\vec{w}=0\:\forall\:\vec{v}\in\mathbb{V}\land\vec{w}\in\mathbb{W}$. 
3. The row space of $A$ is orthogonal to the nullspace. The column space is orthogonal to $\mathbb{N}(A^\text{T})$. 
4. One pair of dimensions adds to $r+(n-r)=n$. The other pair has $r+(m-r)=m$. 
5. Row space and nullspace are orthogonal *complements*: Every $x$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$ splits into $x_\text{row}+x_\text{null}$. 
6. Suppose a space $\mathbb{S}$ has dimension $d$. Then every basis for $\mathbb{S}$ consists of $d$ vectors.
7. If $d$ vectors in $\mathbb{S}$ are independent, they span $\mathbb{S}$. If $d$ vectors span $\mathbb{S}$, they are independent. 

---
Two vectors are orthogonal when their dot product is zero: $\vec{v}\cdot\vec{w}=\vec{v}\,^\text{T}\,\vec{w}$. 
They additionally obey: $\|\vec{v}\|^2+\|\vec{w}\|^2+\|\vec{v}+\vec{w}\|^2$. 

---
*The row space is perpendicular to the nullspace*.
*The column space is perpendicular to the nullspace of $A^\text{T}$.*

***The row space and nullspace are orthogonal subspaces inside of $\,\mathbb{R}^n$***.

**Definition**: Two subspaces $\mathbb{V}$ and $\mathbb{W}$ are **orthogonal** if every vector $\vec{v}\in\mathbb{V}$ is perpendicular to every vector $\vec{w}\in\mathbb{W}$.

---
(Orthogonality is impossible when $\mathrm{dim}\,\mathbb{V}+\mathrm{dim}\,\mathbb{W}>\mathrm{dim}\,(\text{whole space})$. 

---
Every vector $\vec{x}$ in the nullspace is perpendicular to every row of $A$, because $A\vec{x}=\vec{0}$. 
***The nullspace $\mathbb{N}(A)$ and the row space $\mathbb{C}(A^\text{T})$ are orthogonal subspaces of $\mathbb{R}^n$.***

---
Every vector $\vec{y}$ in the nullspace of $A^\text{T}$ is perpendicular to every column of $A$. ***The left nullspace $\mathbb{N}(A^\text{T})$ and the column space $\mathbb{C}(A)$ are orthogonal in $\mathbb{R}^m$. 

---
**Definition** The *orthogonal complement* of a subspace $\mathbb{V}$ contains every vector that is perpendicular to $\mathbb{V}$. This orthogonal subpsace is denoted by $\mathbb{V}^\perp$. 

---
**Fundamental Theorem of Linear Algebra, Part 2**
$\mathbb{N}(A)$ is the orthogonal complement of the row space $\mathbb{C}(A^\text{T})$ (In $\mathbb{R}^n$).
$\mathbb{N}(A^\text{T})$ is the orthogonal complement of the column space $\mathbb{C}(A)$ (in $\mathbb{R}^m$).

---
Every matrix of rank $r$ has an $r\times r$ invertible submatrix, the "pseudoinverse".

Every matrix can be diagonalized, when we choose the right bases for $\mathbb{R}^n$ and $\mathbb{R}^m$. This **Singular Value Decomposition** (SVD) has become extremely important in applicaations.

---
**Review of the Key Ideas**
1. Subspaces $\mathbb{V}$ and $\mathbb{W}$ are orthogonal if every $\vec{v}\in\mathbb{V}$ is orthogonal to every $\vec{w}\in\mathbb{W}$. 
2. $\mathbb{V}$ and $\mathbb{W}$ are "orthogonal complements" if $\mathbb{W}$ contains **all** vectors perpendicular to $\mathbb{V}$ (and vice-versa). Inside $\mathbb{R}^n$, the dimensions of complements $\mathbb{V}$ and $\mathbb{W}$ sum to $n$. 
3. The nullspace $\mathbb{N}(A)$ and the row space $\mathbb{C}(A^\text{T})$ are orthogonal complements, with dimensions $(n-r)+r=n$. Similarly $\mathbb{N}(A^\text{T})$ and $\mathbb{C}(A)$ are orthogonal complements with $(m-r)+r=m$. 
4. Any $n$ independent vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$ span $\mathbb{R}^n$. Any $n$ spanning vectors are independent.
