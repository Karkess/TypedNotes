## 4.2 - Projections

1. The projection of a vector $\vec{b}$ onto the line through $\vec{a}$ is the closest point $p=\vec{a}\,(\vec{a}\,^\text{T}\,\vec{b}/\vec{a}\,^\text{T}\,\vec{a})$.
2. The error $\vec{e}=\vec{b}-\vec{p}$ is perpendicular to $\vec{a}: Right triangle $\vec{b}\,\vec{p}\,\vec{e}$ has $\|p\|^2+\|e\|^2=\|b\|^2$.
3. The **projection** of $\vec{b}$ onto a subspace $\mathbb{S}$ is the closest vector $\vec{p}$ in $\mathbb{S}$; $\vec{b}-\vec{p}$ is orthogonal in $\mathbb{S}$. 
4. $A^\text{T}A$ is invertible (and symmetric) only if $$A has independent columns: $\mathbb{N}(A^\text{T}A)=\mathbb{N}(A)$. 
5. Then the projection of $\vec{b}$ onto the column space of $A$ is the vector $\vec{p}=A(A^\text{T}A)^{-1}A^\text{T}\vec{b}$. 
6. The **projection matrix** onto $\mathbb{C}(A)$ is $P=A(A^\text{T}A)^{-1}A^\text{T}$. It has $\vec{p}=P\vec{b}$ and $P^2=P=P^\text{T}$. 

---
Our goal is to project any $\vec{b}$ onto the column space of any $m\times n$ matrix.

---
**Projection Onto a Line**
The key to projection is orthogonality: The line from $b$ to $p$ is perpendicular to vector $\vec{a}$. 

The projection $p$ will be a multiple of $a$, which we will denote $\hat{x}$. 

The error $e$ is the distance between $\vec{b}$ and the projection $\vec{p}$, so $\vec{e}=\vec{b}-\vec{p}$. 

Projecting $\vec{b}$ onto $\vec{a}$ with error $\vec{e}=\vec{b}-\hat{x}\vec{a}$. $$\vec{a}\cdot(\vec{b}-\hat{x}\,\vec{a})=0\quad\text{or}\quad\vec{a}\cdot\vec{b}-\hat{x}\,\vec{a}\cdot\vec{a}=0.$$$$\hat{x}=\frac{\vec{a}\cdot\vec{b}}{\vec{a}\cdot\vec{a}}=\frac{\vec{a}\,^\text{T}\,\vec{b}}{\vec{a}\,^\text{T}\,\vec{a}}.$$

The projection of $b$ onto the line through $a$ is the vector $\vec{p}=\hat{x}\vec{a}=\frac{\vec{a}\,^\text{T}\,\vec{b}}{\vec{a}\,^\text{T}\,\vec{a}}\vec{a}$.

---
**Projection matrix $P$**
$$\vec{p}=\vec{a}\hat{x}=\vec{a}\frac{\vec{a}\,^\text{T}\,\vec{b}}{\vec{a}\,^\text{T}\,\vec{a}}=P\,\vec{b}$$ when the matrix is $$P=\frac{\vec{a}\vec{a}\,^\text{T}}{\vec{a}\,^\text{T}\,\vec{a}}.$$ Projecting a asecond time doesn't change anything, so $P^2=P$, the diagonal entries of $P$ add up to 1.

**Projection Onto a Subspace**
This section is honestly a little confusing to me in how the book explains it. I feel like my intuitive thinking about it via geometry and ability to follow the algebra would help me follow this explanation but it is not. It's pretty easy for example, to imagine the projection of a line onto a plane. Anyways...

The combination $\vec{p}=\hat{x}_1\vec{a}_1+\dots+\hat{x}_n\vec{a}_n=A\hat{x}$ that is closest to $\vec{b}$ comes from $\hat{x}$:$$\textbf{Find }\hat{x}(n\times1)\quad A^\text{T}(\vec{b}-A\hat{x})=0\:\text{or}\:A^\text{T}A\hat{x}=A^\text{T}\,\vec{b}.$$ This symmetry matrix $A^\text{T}A$ is $n\times n$. It is invertible if the $\vec{a}$'s are linearly independent. The solution is $\hat{x}=(A^\text{T}A)^{-1}A^\text{T}\,\vec{b}$. The **projection** of $\vec{b}$ onto the subspace is $\vec{p}\,$:$$\textbf{Find }\vec{p}(m\times1)\quad\vec{p}=A\hat{x}=A(A^\text{T}A)^{-1}A^\text{T}\,\vec{b}.$$ The next formula picks out the projection matrix that is multiplying $\vec{b}\,$:$$\textbf{Find }P(m\times m)\quad P=A(A^\text{T}A)^{-1}A^\text{T}.$$

These are the same formulas as for the $n=1$ case, we just swapped $\vec{a}$ for $A$. When we would divide by $a$, we take the inverse of $A$. 

After some intensive checking it appears that all of this checks with an example.

**Review of Key Ideas**
1. The projection of $\vec{b}$ onto the line $\vec{a}$ is $\vec{p}=\vec{a}\,\hat{x}=\vec{a}\,(\vec{a}\,^\text{T}\,\vec{b}/\vec{a}\,^\text{T}\,\vec{a}).$
2. The rank one projection matrix $P=\vec{a}\,\vec{a}\,^\text{T}/\vec{a}\,^\text{T}\,\vec{a}$ multiplies $\vec{b}$ to produce $\vec{p}$. 
3. Projecting $\vec{b}$ onto a subspace leaves $\vec{e}=\vec{b}-\vec{p}$ perpendicular to the subspace.
4. When $A$ has full rank $n$, the equation $A^\text{T}\,A\hat{x}=A^\text{T}\,\vec{b}$ leads to $\hat{x}$ and $\vec{p}=A\hat{x}$. 
5. The projection matrix $P=A(A^\text{T}A)^{-1}A^\text{T}$ has $P^\text{T}=P$ and $P^2=P$ and $P\vec{b}=\vec{p}$.
