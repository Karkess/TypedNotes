## 4.3 - Least Squares Approximation
1. Solving $A^\text{T}A\hat{x}=A^\text{T}\,\vec{b}$ gives the projection $\vec{p}=A\hat{x}$ of $\vec{b}$ onto the column space of $A$. 
2. When $A\vec{x}=\vec{b}$ has no solution, $\hat{x}$ is the "least-squares solution": $\|\vec{b}-A\hat{x}\|^2=$ minimum.
3. Setting partial derivatives of $E=\|A\vec{x}-\vec{b}\|^2\,$ to zero $\left(\frac{\partial E}{\partial x_i}=0\right)$ also produces $A^\text{T}A\hat{x}=A^\text{T}\,\vec{b}$. 
4. To fit points $(t_1,b_1),\dots,(t_m,b_m)$ by a straight line, $A$ has columns $(1,\dots,1)$ and $(t_1,\dots,t_m)$. 
5. In that case $A^\text{T}A$ is the $2\times2$ matrix $\begin{bmatrix}m&\sum t_i\\ \sum t_i&\sum t_i^2\end{bmatrix}$ and $A^\text{T}\,\vec{b}$ is the vector $\begin{bmatrix}\sum b_i\\ \sum t_ib_i\end{bmatrix}$. 

---
It often happens that $A\vec{x}=\vec{b}$ has no solution. The usual reason is: *too many equations*. The matrix $A$ has more rows than columns, $m>n$. 

Using our error $e$ we can solve for $\hat{x}$ to find the solution that is closest to the real solution by minimizing the error. When the length of $e$ is as small as possible, we have the "least-squares solution". 

**When $A\vec{x}=\vec{b}$ has no solution, multiply by $A^\text{T}$ and solve $A^\text{T}A\hat{x}=A^\text{T}\,\vec{b}$.**

---
**Minimizing the Error**
To minimize the error, the best $\vec{x}$ is our $\hat{x}$! 

This can be found by geometry: The error $e$ meets the column space of $A$ at $90^\circ$. 
And algebra: Solving $A^\text{T}A\,\hat{x}=A^\text{T}\,\vec{b}$

*The least squares solution $\hat{x}$ makes $E=\|A\vec{x}-\vec{b}\|^2$ as small as possible. 

**The partial derivatives of $\|A\vec{x}-\vec{b}\|^2$ are zero when $A^\text{T}A\hat{x}=A^\text{T}\,\vec{b}$. 

---
**Fitting a Straight Line**

The line $C+Dt$ minimizes $e_1^2+\dots+e_m^2=\|A\vec{x}-\vec{b}\|^2$ when $A^\text{T}A\hat{x}=A^\text{T}\,\vec{b}$:$$A^\text{T}A\,\hat{x}=A^\text{T}\,\vec{b}\quad\quad\begin{bmatrix}b&\sum t_i\\ \sum t_i&\sum t_i^2\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}C\\D\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}\sum b_i\\ \sum t_ib_i\end{bmatrix}.$$

---
**Fitting by a Parabola**
**Least Squares**: The closest parabola $C+Dt+Et^2$ chooses $\hat{x}=(C,D,E)$ to satisfy the three normal equations $A^\text{T}A\hat{x}=A^\text{T}b$. 

---
**Review of the Key Ideas**
1. The least squares solution $\hat{x}$ minimizes $\|Ax-b\|^2=\vec{x}\,^\text{T}A^\text{T}A\vec{x}-2x^\text{T}A^\text{T}\vec{b}+\vec{b}^\text{T}\vec{b}$. This is $E$, the sum of squares of the errors in the $m$ equations $(m>n)$.
2. The best $\hat{x}$ comes from the normal equations $A^\text{T}A\hat{x}=A^\text{T}\,\vec{b}$. $E$ is a minimum.
3. To fit $m$ points by a line $b=C+Dt$, the normal equations give $C$ and $D$. 
4. The heights of the best line are $\vec{p}=(p_1,\dots,p_m)$. The vertical distances to the data points are the error $\vec{e}=(e_1,\dots,e_m)$. A key equation is $A^\text{T}\,\vec{e}=\vec{0}$. 
5. If we try to fit $m$ points by a combination of $n<m$ functions, the $m$ equations $A\vec{x}=\vec{b}$ are generally unsolvable. The $n$ equations $A^\text{T}A\hat{x}=A^\text{T}\,\vec{b}$ give the least squares solution, the combination with the smallest mean square error.
