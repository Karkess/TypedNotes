## 2.3 Elimination Using Matrices
1. The first step multiplies the equations $A\vec{x}=\vec{b}$ by a matrix $E_{21}$ to produce $E_{21}A\vec{x}=E_{21}\vec{b}$.
2. That matrix $E_{21}A$ has a zero in row $2$, column $1$ because $x_1$ is eliminated from equation $2$. 
3. $E_{21}$ is the **identity matrix** minus the multiplier $a_{21}/a_{11}$ in row $2$, column $1$.
4. Matrix-matrix multiplication is $n$ matrix-vector multiplications: $EA=[Ea_1\dots Ea_n]$. 
5. We must multiply $Eb!$ So $E$ is multiplying the **augmented matrix** $[Ab]=[a_1\dots a_n\vec{b}]$.
6. Elimination multiplies $A\vec{x}=\vec{b}$ by $E_{21}, E_{31},\dots,E_{n1}$, then $E_{32},E_{42},\dots,E{n2}$ and so on. 
7. The **row exchange matrix** is not $E_{ij}$, but $P_{ij}$. To find $P_{ij}$, exchange rows $i$ and $j$ of $I$.

---
This section introduces **matrix multiplication** through the use of simple **elimination matrices** $E$. 

$A\vec{x}$ *is a combination of columns of $A$.* To compute each component of $A\vec{x}$. Using the row form of matrix multiplication and use the short form of the equation with "sigma notation":$$\sum^n_{j=1}a_{ij}x_j$$ Einstein shortens this by omitting the $\sum$ and then alternating the indices to say $a^j_ix_j$ with an understanding that repeated indices are to be summed over.

---
The *elementary matrix or elimination matrix* $E_{ij}$ is similar to an identity matrix that has an extra nonzero entry $-\ell$ in the $i,j$ position, example: $$E_{31}=\begin{bmatrix}1&0&0\\0&1&0\\-\ell&0&1\end{bmatrix}$$

---
How do we multiply two matrices? This particular example $E$ subtracts 2 times row 1 from row 2: $$EA=\begin{bmatrix}1&0&0\\-2&1&0\\0&0&1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}2&4&-2\\4&9&-3\\-2&-3&7\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}2&4&-2\\0&1&1\\-2&-3&7\end{bmatrix}$$

For matrix multiplication: 
**Associative law is true**
**Commutative law is false**

For matrix multiplication with a matrix multiplied by another with multiple columbs, say matrix $B$ with columns $b_1,b_2,b_3$: $$AB=A[b_1,b_2,b_3]=[Ab_1,Ab_2,Ab_3]$$

---
We also have another example of a matrix, the **Permutation matrix** $P_{ij}$, which swaps rows $i$ and $j$ of any other matrix.$$P_{23}=\begin{bmatrix}1&0&0\\0&0&1\\0&1&0\end{bmatrix}.$$

---
**The Augmented Matrix**
We can for visual purposes combine a matrix with another to form an augmented matrix, with our $A\vec{b}$ analogy we have: $$[A\,\,\vec{b}\,]=\begin{bmatrix}a_{11}&a_{12}&a_{13}&b_1\\a_{21}&a_{22}&a_{23}&b_2\\a_{31}&a_{32}&a_{33}&b_3\end{bmatrix}.$$ This is usually used for doing elimination calculations where the extra column can be treated quickly with other rows.

---
**Review of the Key Ideas**
1. $A\vec{x}=x_1$ times the column $1+\dots+x_n$ times column $n$, and $(A\vec{x})_i=\sum^n_{j=1}a_{ij}x_j$.
2. Identity matrix = $I$, elimination matrix = $E_{ij}$ using $\ell_ij$, exchange matrix = $P_{ij}$.
3. Multiplying $A\vec{x}=\vec{b}$ by $E_{21}$ subtrats a multiple of $\ell_{21}$ of equation 1 from equation 2. The number $-\ell_{21}$ is the $(2,1)$ entry of the elimination matrix $E_{21}$. 
4. For an augmented matrix $[A\,\,\vec{b}\,]$, that elimination step gives $[E_{21}A\,\,E_{21}\vec{b}\,]$.
