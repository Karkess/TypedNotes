# 2.7 - Transposes and Permutations

1. The transposes of $A\vec{x}$ and $AB$ and $A^{-1}$ are $\vec{x}\,^TA^T$ and $V^TA^T$ and $(A^T)^{-1}$. 
2. The dot product (inner product) is $\vec{x}\cdot\vec{y}=\vec{x}\,^T\:\vec{y}$. This is $(1\times n)(n\times1)=(1\times1)$. The outer product $\vec{x}\:\vec{y}\,^T=$ column times row $=(n\times1)(1\times n)=n\times n$ matrix.
3. The idea behind $A^T$ is that $A\vec{x}\cdot\vec{y}$ equals $\vec{x}\cdot A^T\:\vec{y}$ because $(A\vec{x})^T\:\vec{y}=\vec{x}^TA^T\:\vec{y}=\vec{x}\,^T(A^T\:\vec{y})$
4. A **symmetric matrix** has $S^T=S$ (and the product $A^T=A$ is always symmetric.)
5. An **orthogonal matrix** has $Q^T=Q^{-1}$. The columns of $Q$ are orthogonal unit vectors.
6. A **permutation matrix** $P$ has the same rows as $I$ (in any order). There are $n!$ different orders.
7. Then $P\vec{x}$ puts the components $x_1,x_2,\dots,x_n$ in that new order, and $P\,^T=P\,^{-1}$

---
The **Transpose** $A^T$ turns the rows of $A$ into the columns of $A^T$. $$(A^T\,)_{ij}=A_{ji}.$$ Which has the following properties:$$\textbf{Sum}\quad\text{The transpose of } A+B\text{ is }A^T+B^T$$ $$\textbf{Product}\quad\text{The transpose of }AB\text{ is }(AB)^T=B^TA^T$$ $$\textbf{Inverse}\quad\text{The transpose of }A^{-1}\text{ is }(A^{-1})^T=(A^T)^{-1}$$

---
**The Meaning of Inner Products**

The dot product (inner product) of $\vec{x}$ and $\vec{y}$ can be written more professionally that $\vec{x}\cdot\vec{y}$ as $\vec{x}\,^T\vec{y}$. 

The outer product can be written as $\vec{x}\vec{y}\,^T$. 

---
**Symmetric Matrices**

A *symmetric matrix* has $S^T=S$. This means that $s_{ij}=s_{ji}$.

The products $A^T\,A$ and $AA^T$ and $LDL^T$ are symmetric matrices.

---
**Permutation Matrices**
A permutation matrix $P$ has the rows of the identity $I$ in any order. 

An identity of permutation matrices is that $P^{-1}$ is always the same as $P^T$, thus $$PP^T=I.$$

---
**Review of the Key Ideas**

1. The transpose puts the rows of $A$ into the columns of $A^T$, then $(A^T)_{ij}=A_{ji}$.
2. The transpose of $AB$ is $B^TA^T$. The transpose of $A^{-1}$ is the inverse of $A^T$.
3. The dot product is $\vec{x}\cdot\vec{y}=\vec{x}\,^T\vec{y}$. Then $(A\vec{x})^T\vec{y}$ equals the dot product $\vec{x}^T(A^T\,)\,\vec{y}$.
4. When $S$ is symmetric $(S^T=S)$, its $LDU$ factorization is symmetric: $S=LDL^T$. 
5. A permutation matrix $P$ has a 1 in each row and column, and $P^T=P^{-1}$.
6. There are $n!$ permutation matrices of size $n$. Half even, half odd.
7. If $A$ is invertible then a permutation $P$ will reorder its rows for $PA=LU.$
