## 2.5 - Inverse Matrices

1. If the square matrix $A$ has an inverse, then both $A^{-1}A=I$ and $AA^{-1}=I.$
2. The *algorithm* to test invertibility is elimination: $A$ must have $n$ (nonzero) pivots.
3. The *algebra* test for invertibility is the determinant of $A: \mathrm{det}\,A$ must not be zero. 
4. The *equation* that tests for invertibility is $A\vec{x}=0:\vec{x}=0$ **must be the only solution**. 
5. If $A$ and $B$ (same size) are invertible is $AB\,:(AB)^{-1}=B^{-1}A^{-1}$. 
6. $AA^{-1}=I$ is $n$ equations for $n$ columns of $A^{-1}$. Gauss-Jordan eliminates $[A\,I]$ to $[I\,A^{-1}]$.
7. The last page of the book gives 14 equivalent conditions for square $A$ to be invertible. 

Let's list them here: 
1. $A$ is invertible. (my favorite)
2. The columns are independent.
3. The rows are independent.
4. The determinant is not zero.
5. $A\vec{x}=0$ has one solution $\vec{x}=0$.
6. $A\vec{x}=\vec{b}$ has one solution $\vec{x}=A^{-1}\,\vec{b}$. 
7. $A$ has $n$ (nonzero) pivots). 
8. $A$ has full rank $r=n$.
9. The reduced row echelon form is $R=I$. 
10. The column space is all of $\mathbb{R}^n$.
11. The row space is all of $\mathbb{R}^n$.
12. All eigenvalues are nonzero.
13. $A^TA$ is symmetric positive definite.
14. $A$ has $n$ (positive) singular values.

---
**Definition** The matrix *A* is **invertible** if there exists a matrix $A^{-1}$ that "inverts" $A$:$$\textbf{Two-sided inverse}\quad\quad$A^{-1}=I\text{  and  }AA^{-1}=I.$$

---
The determinant of $2\times2$ matrix $A: \begin{bmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{bmatrix}$ is $ad-bc$. 

---
The inverse of a series of matrix multiplication reverses the order of multiplication:$$(ABC)^{-1}=C^{-1}\,B^{-1}\,A^{-1}\,.$$

---
**Calculating $A^{-1}$ by Gauss-Jordan elimination**. 
The Gauss-Jordan method calculates the inverse by using an augmented matrix with the identity and then elimination to get the original matrix into the identity, example:$$\begin{bmatrix}2&-1&0&1&0&0\\-1&2&-1&0&1&0\\0&-1&2&0&0&1\end{bmatrix}\rightarrow\begin{bmatrix}1&0&0&\frac{3}{4}&\frac{1}{2}&\frac{1}{4}\\0&1&0&\frac{1}{2}&1&\frac{1}{2}\\0&0&1&\frac{1}{4}&\frac{1}{2}&\frac{1}{4}\end{bmatrix}.$$$$\textbf{Multiply}\quad[A\,\,I]\textbf{ by }A^{-1}\textbf{ to get }[I\,\,A^{-1}]$$

---
**Singular Versus Invertible**
What makes a matrix invertible? $A^{-1}$ exists exactly when $A$ has a full set of $n$ pivots. 
$$\text{If }AC=I\text{ then }CA=I\text{ and }C=A^{-1}\,.$$

---
**Recognizing an Invertible Matrix**
Diagonally dominant matrices are invertible: Each $a_{ii}$ on the diagonal is larger than the total sum along the rest of row $i$ on every row.

---
**Review of the Key Ideas** 

1. The inverse matrix gives $AA^{-1}=I$ and $A^{-1}\,A=I$.
2. $A$ is invertible if and only if it has $n$ pivots (row exchanges allowed). 
3. *Important*. If $A\vec{x}=0$ for a nonzero vector $\vec{x}$, then $A$ has no inverse. 
4. The inverse of $AB$ is the reverse product $B^{-1}\,A^{-1}$, and $(ABC)^{-1}=C^{-1}\,B^{-1}\,A^{-1}$. 
5. The Gauss-Jordan method solves $AA^{-1}=I$ to find the $n$ columns of $A^{-1}$. The augmented matrix $[A\,I]$ is row reduced to $[I\,A^{-1}]$. 
6. Diagonally dominant matrices are invertible. Each $\lvert a_{ii}\rvert$ dominates its row.
