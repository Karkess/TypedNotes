# 2.4 - Rules for Matrix Operations
1. Matrices $A$ with $n$ columns multiply matrices $B$ with $n$ rows: $A_{m\times n}\,B_{n\times p}=C_{m\times p}\,$. 
2. Each entry in $AB=C$ is a dot product: $C_{ij}=(\text{row }i\text{ of }A)\cdot(\text{column }j\text{ of }B).$
3. The rule is chosen so that $AB$ times $C$ equals $A$ times $BC$, and $(AB)x=A(Bx)$. 
4. More ways to compute $AB$: ($A$ times the columns of $B$)(rows of $A$ times $B$)(columns times rows).
5. It is not usually true that $AB=BA$. In most cases $A$ *does not commute with* $B$. 
6. Matrices can be multiplied by *blocks*: $A=[A_1\:A_2]$ times $B=\begin{bmatrix}B_1\\B_2\end{bmatrix}$ is $A_1B_1+A_2B_2$. 

---
To multiply $AB$: If $A$ has $n$ columns, $B$ must have $n$ rows.

**Fundamental Law of Matrix Multiplication**: *$AB$ times $C$ equals $A$ times $BC$*.

For matrix multiplication, where $A$ has dimension $m\times n$ and $B$ has $n\times p$:$$(m\times n)(n\times p)=(m\times p).$$

*The entry in row $i$ and column $j$ of $AB$ is* $(\text{row }i\text{ of }A)\cdot(\text{column }j\text{ of }B)\,.$

---
**The Second and Third Ways: Rows and Columns**

2. Matrix $A$ times every column of $B$: $A[b_1,\dots,b_p]=[Ab_1,\dots,Ab_p]$. 

3. Every row of $A$ times every matrix $B$: $[\text{row }i\text{ of }A]\begin{bmatrix}b_{11}&b_{12}&b_{13}\\b_{21}&b_{22}&b_{23}\\b_{31}&b_{32}&b_{33}\end{bmatrix}=[\text{row }i\text{ of }AB].$

---
**The Fourth Way: Columns Multiple Rows**

4. Multiply columns 1 to $n$ of $A$ times rows $1$ to $n$ of $B$. Add those matrices. 

I feel like this is the one I've always used and I struggle to remember it. I feel like the dot product is way easier for me to see, remember, and calculate. 

---
**The Laws for Matrix Operations**

The matrix addition laws: $$A+B=B+A\quad\quad(\text{commutative law})$$$$c(A+B)=cA+cB\quad(\text{distributive law})$$$$A+(B+C)=(A+B)+C\quad(\text{associative law})$$

The matrix multiplication laws: $$A(B+C)=AB+AC\quad\text{(distributive law from the left)}$$$$(A+B)C=AC+BC\quad\text{(distributive law from the right)}$$$$A(BC)=(AB)(C)\quad(\text{associative law for }ABC)$$

The commutative law only holds for some special matrices, and is usually broken for multiplication: $$AB\neq BA.$$

---
**Review of key ideas**
1. The (i,j) entry of $AB$ is (row $i$ of $A$) $\cdot$ (column $j$ of $B$).
2. an $m$ by $n$ matrix times an $n$ by $p$ matrix uses $mnp$ separate multiplications.
3. $A$ times $BC$ equals $AB$ times $C$.
4. $AB$ is also the sum of these $n$ matrices: (column $j$ of $A$) times (row $j$ of $B$).
5. Block multiplication is allowed when block shapes match correctly.
6. Block elimination produces the *Schur complement* $D-CA^{-1}B$.
