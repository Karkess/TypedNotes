# Chapter 2 - Solving Linear Equations
## Chapter 2.1 - Vectors and Linear Equations

1. The **column picture of** $A\vec{x}=\vec{b}$: a combination of $n$ columns of $A$ produce the vector $\vec{b}$. 
2. This is a vector equation $A\vec{x}=x_1\vec{a_1}+\dots+x_n\vec{a_n}=\vec{b}$: the columns of $A$ are $a_1,a_2,\dots,a_n$.
3. When $\vec{b}=0$, a combination $A\vec{x}$ of the columns is *zero*: one possibility is $x=(0,\dots,0)$. 
4. The **row picture of** $A\vec{x}=\vec{b}$: $m$ equations from $m$ rows give $m$ planes meeting at $\vec{x}$. 
5. A dot product gives the equation of each plane: (**row 1**)$\,\cdot\,\vec{x}=b_1,\cdots,(\textbf{row }m)\,\cdot\,\vec{x}=b_m$
6. When $\vec{b}=0$, all of the planes (**row** $i$)$\,\cdot\,\vec{x}=0$ go through the center point $x=(0,0,\dots,0)$. 

---
The central problem of linear algebra is to solve systems of linear equations. We will start with a simple set:$$\begin{array}{1}\textbf{Two equations}\\\textbf{Two unknowns}\end{array}\quad\quad\begin{array}{1}x-2y=1\\3x+2y=11\end{array}$$ 

---
**ROWS:$\quad$** ***The row picture shows two lines meeting at a single point (the solution).***

**COLUMNS:$\quad$** ***The column picture combines the column vectors on the left side to produce the vector $\vec{b}$ on the right side.***

---
$$\textbf{Scalar multiplication}\quad3\begin{bmatrix}1\\3\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}3\\9\end{bmatrix}$$
$$\textbf{Vector addition}\quad\begin{bmatrix}3\\9\end{bmatrix}+\begin{bmatrix}-2\\2\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}1\\11\end{bmatrix}.$$
$$\textbf{Linear Combination}\quad3\begin{bmatrix}1\\3\end{bmatrix}+\begin{bmatrix}-2\\2\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}1\\11\end{bmatrix}.$$

---
$$\textbf{Coefficient matrix}\quad A=\begin{bmatrix}1&-2\\3&2\end{bmatrix}.$$
$$\textbf{Matrix equation}\quad\begin{bmatrix}1&-2\\3&2\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}x\\y\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}1\\11\end{bmatrix}.$$

---
In three dimensions (three equations with three unknowns, here), we can imagine for the 'row picture', that we have two of the planes intersectings to make a line, and then a third plane intersects at a point which is the solution. The column picture is similar tip-to-tail stuff as in 2D but in 3D. 

---
What does it mean to "multiple $A$ times $\vec{x}$"?

*Multiplication by rows*: $A\vec{x}$ comes from **dot products**: $$A\vec{x}=\begin{bmatrix}(\text{row 1})\cdot\vec{x}\\(\text{row 2})\cdot\vec{x}\\(\text{row 3})\cdot\vec{x}\end{bmatrix}.$$

*Multiplication by columns*: $A\vec{x}$ is a combination of column vectors: $$A\vec{x}=x\,(\text{column 1})+y\,(\text{column 2})+z\,(\text{column 3}).$$

---
***This book sees $A\vec{x}$ as a combination of the columns of $A$.***

---
$$\textbf{Identity Matrix:}\quad I=\begin{bmatrix}1&0&0\\0&1&0\\0&0&1\end{bmatrix}\text{ always yields }\:I\vec{x}=\vec{x.}$$

---
**Matrix Notation**
We denote row 5, column 7 in a matrix as $a_{57}=A(5,7)$, or generally $a_{ij}=A(i,j)$: $$A=\begin{bmatrix}a_{11}&a_{12}\\a_{21}&a_{22}\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}A(1,1)&A(1,2)\\A(2,1)&A(2,2)\end{bmatrix}.$$

---
**REVIEW OF KEY IDEAS**
1. The basic operations on vectors are the multiplication $c\vec{v}$ and vector addition $\vec{v}+\vec{w}$.
2. Together those give *linear combinations* $c\vec{v}+d\vec{w}$. 
3. Matrix-vector multiplication $A\vec{x}$ can be computed by dot products, one row at a time, but $A\vec{x}$ must be understood as a *combination of columns of $A$.*
4. Column picture $A\vec{x}=\vec{b}$ asks for a combination of columns to produce $\vec{b}$.
5. Row picture: Each equation in $A\vec{x}=\vec{b}$ gives a line $(n=2)$ or a plane $n=3$ or a "hyperplane" $(n>3)$. The intersections are the solutions, if any.
