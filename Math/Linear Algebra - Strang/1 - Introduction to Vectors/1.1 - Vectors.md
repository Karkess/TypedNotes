# Chapter 1
## Introduction to Vectors

The heart of linear algebra is the addition and multiplication of vectors, combined together in one as a **linear combination**.

**Linear combination**
$$c\vec{v}+d\vec{w}=c\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix}+d\begin{bmatrix}2\\3\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}c+2d\\c+3d\end{bmatrix}.$$

## 1.1 Vectors and Linear Combinations
1. $3\vec{v}+5\vec{w}$ is a typical **linear combination** $c\vec{v}+d\vec{w}$ of the vectors $\vec{v}$ and $\vec{w}$. 
2. For $\vec{v}=\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix}$ and $\vec{w}=\begin{bmatrix}2\\3\end{bmatrix}$ that combination is $3\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix}+5\begin{bmatrix}2\\3\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}3+10\\3+15\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}13\\18\end{bmatrix}$. 
3. The combinations $c\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix}+d\begin{bmatrix}2\\3\end{bmatrix}$ fill the whole $xy$ plane. They produce every $\begin{bmatrix}x\\y\end{bmatrix}$. 
4. The combinations $c\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\\1\end{bmatrix}+d\begin{bmatrix}2\\3\\4\end{bmatrix}$ fill a **plane** in $xyz$ space. 
5. But $\:\begin{array}{r}c+2d=1\\c+3d=0\\c+4d=0\end{array}$ has no solution because it's right side $\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\\0\end{bmatrix}$ is not on that plane.

---
$$\textbf{Column Vector }\vec{v}\quad\quad\vec{v}=\begin{bmatrix}v_1\\v_2\end{bmatrix}\quad\begin{array}{1}v_1=&\text{first component of }\vec{v}\\v_2=&\text{second component of }\vec{v}\end{array}$$

---
$$\begin{array}{1}\textbf{VECTOR}\\\textbf{ADDITION}\end{array}\quad\vec{v}=\begin{bmatrix}v_1\\v_2\end{bmatrix}\quad\text{and}\quad\vec{w}=\begin{bmatrix}w_1\\w_2\end{bmatrix}\quad\text{add to}\quad\vec{v}+\vec{w}=\begin{bmatrix}v_1+w_1\\v_2+w_2\end{bmatrix}.$$

---
$$\begin{array}{1}\textbf{SCALAR}\\\textbf{MULTIPLICATION}\end{array}\quad2\vec{v}=\begin{bmatrix}2v_1\\2v_2\end{bmatrix}=\vec{v}+\vec{v}\quad\quad-\vec{v}=\begin{bmatrix}-v_1\\-v_2\end{bmatrix}.$$

---
*The sum of $c\vec{v}$ and $d\vec{w}$ is a linear combination $c\vec{v}+d\vec{w}$.*

---
From now on $\vec{v}=\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\\-1\end{bmatrix}$ is also written as $\vec{v}=(1,1,-1)$. Note that this is ***not*** a row vector. 

---
Consider combinations of vectors. 
1. The combinations of $c\vec{u}\,$ fill a **line** through $(0,0,0)$.
2. The combinations of $c\vec{u}+d\vec{v}\,$ fill a **plane** through $(0,0,0)$.
3. The combinations of $c\vec{u}+d\vec{v}+e\vec{w}\,$ fill **three-dimensional space**.
