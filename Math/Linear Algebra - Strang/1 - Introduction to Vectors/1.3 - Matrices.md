# 1.3 Matrices
1. $A=\begin{bmatrix}1&2\\3&4\\5&6\end{bmatrix}$ is a $3$ by $2$ matrix: $m=3$ rows and $n=2$ columns.
2. $A\vec{x}=\begin{bmatrix}1&2\\3&4\\5&6\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}x_1\\x_2\end{bmatrix}$ is a **combination of the columns** $A\vec{x}=x_1\begin{bmatrix}1\\3\\5\end{bmatrix}+x_2\begin{bmatrix}2\\4\\6\end{bmatrix}$. 
3. The components of $A\vec{x}$ are the dot products of $A$ with the vector $x$:$$\textbf{Row at a time}\quad\quad\begin{bmatrix}1&2\\3&4\\5&6\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}7\\8\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}1\cdot7+2\cdot8\\3\cdot7+4\cdot8\\5\cdot7+6\cdot8\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}23\\53\\83\end{bmatrix}$$
4. Equations in matrix form $A\vec{x}=\vec{b}:\begin{bmatrix}2&5\\3&6\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}x_1\\x_2\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}b_1\\b_2\end{bmatrix}$ replaces $\begin{array}{1}2x_1+5x_2=b_1\\3x_1+7x_2=b_2\end{array}.$
5. The solution to $A\vec{x}=\vec{b}$ can be written as $\vec{x}=A^{-1}\,\vec{b}$, but some matrices don't allow $A^{-1}$. 

---
$$\textbf{Three vectors}\quad\vec{u}=\begin{bmatrix}1\\-1\\0\end{bmatrix}\quad\vec{v}=\begin{bmatrix}0\\1\\-1\end{bmatrix}\quad\vec{w}=\begin{bmatrix}0\\0\\1\end{bmatrix}.$$

$$\begin{array}{1}\textbf{Combination}\\\textbf{of the vectors}\end{array}\quad x_1\begin{bmatrix}1\\-1\\0\end{bmatrix}+x_2\begin{bmatrix}0\\1\\-1\end{bmatrix}+x_3\begin{bmatrix}0\\0\\1\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}x_1\\x_2-x_1\\x_3-x_2\end{bmatrix}.$$

$$\begin{array}{1}\textbf{Matrix times vector}\\\textbf{Combination of columns}\end{array}\quad Ax=\begin{bmatrix}1&0&0\\-1&1&0\\0&-1&1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}x_1\\x_2\\x_3\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}x_1\\x_2-x_1\\x_3-x_2\end{bmatrix}.$$

$$\begin{array}{1}Ax\textbf{ is also}\\\textbf{dot products}\\\textbf{with rows}\end{array}\quad Ax=\begin{bmatrix}1&0&0\\-1&1&0\\0&-1&1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}x_1\\x_2\\x_3\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}(1,0,0)\cdot(x_1,x_2,x_3)\\(-1,1,0)\cdot(x_1,x_2,x_3)\\(0,-1,1)\cdot(x_1,x_2,x_3)\end{bmatrix}.$$

---
Thus far we have looked at equations $Ax=b$. Now, we will look at their solutions $x=A^{-1}\,b$. 
$$\begin{array}{1}\textbf{Equations}\\Ax=\vec{b}\end{array}\quad\begin{aligned}x_1&=b_2\\-x_1+x_2&=b_2\\-x_2+x_3&=b_3\end{aligned}$$
$$\begin{array}{1}\textbf{Solution}\\x=A^{-1}\,\vec{b}\end{array}\quad\quad\quad\begin{aligned}x_1&=b_1\\x_2&=b_1+b_2\\x_3&=b_1+b_2+b_3\end{aligned}$$

**Cyclic Differences** 
This gives an example of a cyclic difference matrix:$$C\vec{x}=\begin{bmatrix}1&0&-1\\-1&1&0\\0&-1&1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}x_1\\x_2\\x_3\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}x_1-x_3\\x_2-x_1\\x_3-x_2\end{bmatrix}=\vec{b}.$$These have either infinitely many or no solutions.

---
**Independence and Dependence**
If we look at three vectors, $\vec{u}, \vec{v}, \vec{w}$ and we want to determine if they are linearly independent or not, we see if the third vector, let's say $\vec{w}$ lies in the plane on $\vec{u}\times\vec{v}$. That is, if $\vec{w}$ is a linear combination of $\vec{u}$ and $\vec{v}$. $$\vec{u}+\vec{v}+\vec{w}=0;$$$$\vec{w}=-\vec{u}-\vec{v}.$$

Note:
Independent columns: $A\vec{x}=0$ has one solution. $A$ is an **invertible matrix**.
Dependent columns: $C\vec{x}=0$ has many solutions. $C$ is a **singular matrix**.
