## 5.2 - Permutations and Cofactors
1. $2\times2: ad-bc$ has $2!$ terms with $\pm$ signs. $$n\times n:\det{A}\mathbf{ adds }n! terms with $\pm$ signs. 
2. For $n=3, \det{A}$ adds $3!=6$ termsTwo terms are $+a_{12}a_{23}a_{31}$ and $-a_{13}a_{22}a_{31}$. Rows 1,2,3 and columns 1,2,3 appear once each term. 
3. That minus sign came because the column order $3,2,1$ needs one exchange to recover $1,2,3$. 
4. The six terms include $+a_{11}a_{22}a_{33}-a_{11}a_{23}a_{32}=a_{11}(a_{22}a_{33}-a_{23}a_{32})=a_{11}(\textbf{cofactor}\,C_{11})$
5. Always $\det{A}=a_{11}C_{11}+a_{12}C_{12}+\dots+a_{1n}C_{1n}$. Cofactors are determinates of size $n-1$. 

---
The determinant can be found in three ways: *pivots, big formula, and cofactors*. 

---
**The Pivot Formula**
For the pivot formula, we have: $$(\det{P})(\det{A})=(\det{L})(\det{U}).$$

We turn the matrix into an upper triangular matrix and take the product of all of the pivots. We also take the determinant of the permutation matrix if we did row swaps. 

---
**The Big Formula for Determinates**
This formula has $n!$ terms. 
$\det{A}=$ sum over all $n!$ column permutations, $P=(\alpha,\beta,\dots,\omega)$ $$=\sum(\det{P})a_{1\alpha}a_{2\beta}\dots a_{n\omega}=\textbf{BIG FORMULA}.$$

---
**Determinant by Cofactors**
This is the classic way I've always used, and how I remembered the cross product formula. This is just the isolate each of the values in the row row and do a lil x multiply (down right-down left) and the flip the sign each time, for the 3x3. Break a 4x4 down into 3x3s. This is a slightly simplified version of the big formula. 

Just use the pivot one.

---
1. With no row exchanges, $\det{A}$=(product of pivots). In the upper left corner of $A$, $\det{A}_k$=(product of first $k$ pivots).
2. Every term in the big formula uses each row and column once. Half of hte $n!$ terms have plus signs (when $\det{P}=+1$), the other half have minus signs.
3. The cofactor $C_{ij}$ is $(-1)^{i+j}$ times the smaller determinant that omits row $i$ and column $j$ (because $a_{ij}$ uses that row and column).
4. The determinant is the dot product of any row of $A$ with its row of cofactors. When a row of $A$ has a lot of zeros, we only need a few cofactors.
