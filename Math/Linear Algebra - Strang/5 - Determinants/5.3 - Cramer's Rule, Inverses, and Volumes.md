## 5.3 - Cramer's Rule, Inverses, and Volumes
1. $A^{-1}$ equals $C^\text{T}/\det{A}$. Then $(A^{-1})_{ij}=$ cofactor $C_{ji}$ divided by the determinant of $A$. 
2. **Cramer's Rule** computer $\vec{x}=A^{-1}\,\vec{b}$ from $x_j=\det{(\text{A with column }j\text{ changed to }\vec{b})}$
3. Area of a parallelogram = $|ad-bc|$ if the four corners are $(0,0), (a,b), (c,d),$ and $(a+c,b+d)$. 
4. Volume of box = $|\det{A}|$ if the rows of $A$ (or the columns of $A$) give the sides of the vox.
5. The cross product $\vec{w}=\vec{u}\times\vec{v}$ is $\det{\begin{bmatrix}i&j&k\\u_1&u_2&u_3\\v_1&v_2&v_3\end{bmatrix}}$. 

---
Cramer's Rule solves $A\vec{x}=\vec{b}$. 

**Cramer's Rule** If $\det{A} is not zero, $A\vec{x}=\vec{b}$ is solved by determinants: $$x_1=\frac{\det{B_1}}{\det{A}}\quad x_2=\frac{\det{B_2}}{\det{A}}\quad\dots\quad x_n\frac{\det{B_2}}{\det{A}}.$$ The matrix $B_j$ has the $j$th column of $A$ replaced by the vector $\vec{b}$. 

The determinant of each $B_j$ in Cramer's Rule is a cofactor of $A$. 

---
The $i,j$ entry of $A^{-1}$ is the cofactor $C_{ji}$ divided by $\det{A}$:$$\textbf{Formula for }A^{-1}\quad(A^{-1})_{ij}=\frac{C_{ji}}{\det{A}}\quad\text{and}\quad A^{-1}=\frac{C^\text{T}}{\det{A}}.$$

---
**Area of a Triangle**
A triangle with corners $(x_1,y_1)$ and $(x_2,y_2)$ and $(x_3,y_3)$ has $\textbf{area}=\frac{\textbf{determinant}}{2}$. 

---
**The cross product**
We know plenty about this. It's a special case that is generalized in our geometric algebra studies.

---
**Triple Product = Determinant = Volume**
$$(\vec{u}\times\vec{c})\cdot\vec{w}=\text{volume}.$$

This is literally just the cross product formula, still, except instead of the $i,j,k$ being directions we multiply by them like the dot product.

---
**Review of the Key Ideas**
1. Cramer's Rule solves $A\vec{x}=\vec{b}$ by ratios like $x_1=|B_1|/|A|=|ba_2\dots a_n|/|A|.$
2. When $C$ is the cofactor matrix for $A$, the inverse is $A^{-1}=C^\text{T}/\det{A}.$
3. The vvolume of a box is $|\det{A}|$, when the box edges are the rows of $A$. 
4. Area and volume are needed to change variables in double and triple integrals.
5. In $\mathbb{R}^3$, the cross product $\vec{u}\times\vec{v}$ is perpendicular to $\vec{u}$ and $\vec{v}$. Notice $\hat{i}\times\hat{j}=\hat{k}$.
