# Chapter 1 - Fundamental Concepts
## 1.1 The Stern-Gerlach Experiment 
The Stern-Gerlach experiment is a famous experiment where silver (Ag) atoms are heated in an oven with a small hole through which some of the silver atoms escape and then are collimated before passing through an inhomogeneous magnetic field produced by pole pieces, after which the atoms hit a detector.

The classical prediction for the outcome of this experiment is a distribution of deflections, but the reality of the outcome is that there were two distinct orientations the atoms took. One corresponding to spin "up" and one corresponding to spin "down".

### 1.1.2 Sequential Stern-Gerlach Experiments 

The sequential Stern-Gerlach takes one of the distribution of spin up and spin down beams, lets say spin "up" in $z$ and, then runs them through another magnet oriented in $z$: $$S_z+\rightarrow B_z\rightarrow\text{ all }S_z+$$Then they tried orienting them in a different direction: $$S_z+\rightarrow B_x\rightarrow 50/50\,\text{ for }\,S_x+/\,S_x\,-$$ So they got half and half again in both directions along $B_z$. Then they tried going back to $z$ orientation: $$S_z\rightarrow B_x\rightarrow B_z\rightarrow 50/50\,\text{ for }\,S_z+/S_z\,-$$So the particles have seemingly 'forgotten' which orientation they belonged to. This demonstrates a 'destruction of information'.
